Common issues Beginner-friendly
  • Flat loaf: Under-fermented, over-proofed, weak shaping, or too low an oven temp are the usual suspects.

1. Starter & culture


              

                
                

                  

                    

                      Sourdough uses wild yeast and friendly bacteria instead of commercial yeast. Natural fermentation
                      creates a tangy flavor, a chewy crust, and an open, airy crumb. Whole-grain flours add extra
                      nuttiness and depth.
                    

                  

                

              


              

                
                

                  

                    

                      Mix equal weights of flour and water in a clean jar. Leave it loosely covered at room temperature.
                      Every day, discard a portion and feed the remainder with fresh flour and water. Within 1â€“3 weeks,
                      it should smell pleasantly sour, bubble reliably, and rise after feedings.
                    

                  

                

              


              

                
                

                  

                    

                      Building a new starter can take anywhere from a week to several weeks. Early on you may see bubbles
                      but very little rise â€” this â€œquiet phaseâ€ is normal. Keep feeding on schedule and keep it warm; once
                      a stable yeast and bacteria community forms, it will begin to rise predictably.
                    

                  

                

              


              

                
                

                  

                    

                      Rye and whole-wheat flours are nutrient-rich and tend to kick-start fermentation faster. Unbleached
                      all-purpose flour works well once the starter is established. Avoid bleached flour if possible, and
                      use water without strong chlorine â€” letting tap water sit out overnight usually does the trick.
                    

                  

                

              


              

                
                

                  

                    

                      A clear glass jar makes it easy to see bubbles and how much the starter has risen. Cover loosely so
                      gas can escape: a cloth, loose lid, or plastic wrap works. Avoid sealing it airtight while itâ€™s
                      actively fermenting.
                    

                  

                

              


              

                
                

                  

                    

                      A common feeding ratio is 1:1:1 by weight (starter : flour : water). At room temperature, many
                      starters do well with one or two feedings per day. Higher ratios like 1:5:5 or 1:10:10 give the
                      microbes more food and can help tame an overly acidic or fast-rising starter.
                    

                  

                

              


              

                
                

                  

                    

                      Discard keeps your starter from growing endlessly and helps maintain the right balance of acidity
                      and yeast. You can use discard in pancakes, waffles, crackers, biscuits, muffins, and more â€” or
                      compost it if youâ€™re not in the mood to cook.
                    

                  

                

              


              

                
                

                  

                    

                      A ready starter roughly doubles in volume after feeding, looks bubbly and spongy, and smells tangy
                      and pleasantly yeasty. Many bakers also look for a consistent daily rise-and-fall rhythm. The float
                      test can be a clue, but predictable behavior is more important than one spoonful in water.
                    

                  

                

              


              

                
                

                  

                    

                      Especially in the first 7â€“14 days, itâ€™s common to see bubbles without much rise. Keep feeding on
                      schedule, keep it warm, and consider switching to a stronger flour like rye or whole wheat for a few
                      feeds. Over time, the yeast population usually catches up and the rise improves.
                    

                  

                

              


              

                
                

                  

                    

                      Keep it at warm room temperature, feed consistently, and stick to a ratio like 1:1:1 or 1:2:2 until
                      it reliably doubles within a few hours. For very acidic or sleepy starters, higher-ratio feeds like
                      1:10:10 can help reset the balance.
                    

                  

                

              


              

                
                

                  

                    

                      For longer breaks, feed your starter, let it start to rise for an hour or so, then refrigerate it
                      with a loose lid. If you bake often, feeding it about once a week in the fridge keeps it in good
                      shape. A healthy starter can live in cold storage for many months as long as it stays mold-free.
                    

                  

                

              


              

                
                

                  

                    

                      If you see a layer of liquid on top, stir it back in or pour it off, discard down to a small
                      amount, and resume feeding with fresh flour and water. It may take a few feedings for it to become
                      bubbly and active again. If there is mold, or the starter is streaked pink or orange, itâ€™s safest to
                      start over.
                    

                  

                

              


              

                
                

                  

                    

                      Hooch is the dark or grayish liquid that forms when your starter runs out of food. You can pour it
                      off for a milder flavor or stir it back in for extra tang. If youâ€™re seeing hooch frequently, itâ€™s a
                      sign your starter needs more frequent feedings or cooler temperatures.
                    

                  

                

              


              

                
                

                  

                    

                      Your starter is the ongoing culture you maintain. A levain is a
                      one-time build made from a portion of starter mixed with flour and water from the recipe. Itâ€™s used
                      to leaven a specific dough. Preferment is a broad term that includes levain as well
                      as poolish, biga, and similar builds.
                    

                  

                

              

            


            
            

              
2. Planning & mixing


              

                
                

                  

                    

                      Many home bakers use around 113 g (about Â½ cup) of active starter for a standard loaf, but recipes
                      vary. More starter speeds up fermentation and shortens the timeline; less starter slows things down
                      and can deepen flavor. Adjust the water in your dough to account for how thick or thin your starter
                      is.
                    

                  

                

              


              

                
                

                  

                    

                      Autolyse is when you mix just flour and water and let the dough rest before adding starter and salt.
                      This hydrates the flour, encourages gluten development, and can make the dough easier to handle. Itâ€™s
                      optional but especially helpful with whole-grain or higher-hydration doughs.
                    

                  

                

              


              

                
                

                  

                    

                      Sourdough dough is often mixed gently and strengthened with stretch-and-fold or coil folds during
                      bulk fermentation instead of heavy kneading. High-hydration doughs usually need more rounds of folds
                      to build strength. Wet your hands instead of adding more flour to tame stickiness.
                    

                  

                

              

            


            
            

              
3. Bulk, shaping & proofing


              

                
                

                  

                    

                      Thereâ€™s no single â€œding!â€ moment. During bulk, the dough should feel smoother, stronger, and gain
                      noticeable volume without collapsing. For final proof, the shaped dough should feel puffy and aerated
                      but still able to hold its shape. Practice, temperature awareness, and note-taking help you dial in
                      timing for your kitchen.
                    

                  

                

              


              

                
                

                  

                    

                      Sticky dough usually means high hydration, under-developed gluten, or both. Try slightly reducing
                      the water next time and make sure you give the dough enough folds and rest time. Over-proofed dough
                      can also turn sticky and lose structure.
                    

                  

                

              


              

                
                

                  

                    

                      This often comes from under- or over-fermentation, or from shaping that doesnâ€™t build enough surface
                      tension. Make sure the dough feels strong at the end of bulk, use tight but gentle shaping, and
                      consider lowering hydration if youâ€™re consistently getting puddles instead of domes.
                    

                  

                

              

            


            
            

              
4. Baking & crumb


              

                
                

                  

                    

                      Dense or gummy crumb can mean under-fermentation, a weak starter, too much water, or under-baking.
                      Make sure your starter is truly active, allow the dough to rise fully during bulk and proof, and
                      bake long enough for the interior to set before slicing.
                    

                  

                

              


              

                
                

                  

                    

                      For more tang, use cooler fermentation temperatures, more whole grain, longer
                      ferments, and consider stirring hooch back into the starter. For less sour, feed
                      the starter more often, keep it warmer, and shorten bulk and proof times so the acids donâ€™t build up
                      as much.
                    

                  

                

              


              

                
                

                  

                    

                      An open crumb usually comes from the combo of proper fermentation, good dough strength, and careful
                      handling. Gradually increase hydration as your skills improve, build strength with folds, avoid
                      knocking all the gas out during shaping, and push bulk fermentation far enough without overdoing it.
                    

                  

                

              

            


            
            

              
5. Storage & equipment care


              

                
                

                  

                    

                      Once cool, keep bread at room temperature in a paper bag, bread box, or loosely wrapped to avoid
                      trapping steam. For longer storage, slice the loaf and freeze the slices in a bag; you can toast
                      them straight from frozen for up to a couple of months.
                    

                  

                

              


              

                
                

                  

                    

                      Let the banneton dry completely after use, then brush out excess flour. Avoid washing it with water
                      unless absolutely necessary; moisture encourages mold. Cloth liners can be washed without soap and
                      air-dried fully before storage.
